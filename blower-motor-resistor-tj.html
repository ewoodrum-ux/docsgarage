If your TJ's blower is stuck on high, silent no matter the setting, or only works on one speed, the resistor is the first suspect — but it's not always the root cause. I chased this exact problem on my own '97 4.0L, and a burned resistor turned out to be a symptom, not the disease. Below are the parts I'd actually buy, plus the checks I ran before I bought them.

Signs Your Resistor Is Bad

Most Common Blower only runs on high speed, regardless of dial position — classic burned-out resistor coil.
Electrical A burning smell near the HVAC housing under the dash, or visible scorch marks on the connector.
Wiring Blower cuts out intermittently — often a melted pigtail connector rather than the resistor itself.
Root Cause Check Resistor melts again after replacement — almost always means the blower motor is drawing too much current and cooking the new part.
From the bench Melted resistors are frequently a symptom of a failing blower motor, not a standalone failure. If you replace the resistor and it melts again within a few weeks, stop buying resistors and test the motor's amp draw. A tired motor pulls more current than the resistor coil is rated for, and it'll keep cooking new ones.

Quick Diagnosis Before You Buy

  1. Pull the blower motor fuse/relay and inspect for signs of overheating in the fuse box.
  2. Locate the resistor (passenger side of the HVAC housing, under the dash) and check for melted plastic or a burnt smell.
  3. Unplug the pigtail connector — if the terminals are discolored, warped, or loose, replace the pigtail along with the resistor.
  4. With the resistor unplugged, test blower speeds. If it only ever runs on high, the resistor circuit is confirmed as the fault.
  5. If a new resistor fails again quickly, test blower motor current draw with a multimeter before buying a third one.
