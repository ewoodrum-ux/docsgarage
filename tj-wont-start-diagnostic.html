Jeep TJ Won't Start? Here's the Order to Check Things

A field-tested crank-no-start triage path for the 4.0L, built from actually chasing one down harness to sensor.

Crank-no-start on a 4.0L TJ is almost never one thing — it's usually a chain of small failures that all have to be ruled out in order. Working it randomly wastes time and parts money. Below is the same triage sequence I used on my own '97, moving from the fastest checks to the deepest ones.
⚕ Triage rule: fix the fastest, cheapest thing to rule out first. Don't replace a $200 part to test a $10 theory.

Stage 1: Fuel Delivery

Check First

Is fuel actually reaching the rail?

  • Listen for the fuel pump prime (2-second hum) when you turn the key to ON
  • Check fuel pump relay and fuse before assuming the pump itself is dead
  • Verify fuel pressure at the rail with a gauge (TJ 4.0L should hold spec pressure with key on, engine off)
  • Inspect the fuel filter/pump strainer if the Jeep has sat unused for a long stretch

Stage 2: Spark

Check Second

Is the coil actually firing?

  • Pull a plug, ground it against the block, and crank — look for a strong blue spark
  • Check the crank position sensor connector for corrosion or a loose pin (common failure point)
  • Inspect the coil pack and plug wires for cracking, especially if the engine bay runs hot
  • Verify the ASD (Automatic Shutdown) relay is actually energizing — no ASD signal means no spark and no injector pulse, even with good fuel pressure

Stage 3: Cam/Crank Sync

Check Third — Often the Real Culprit

Does the PCM see a valid sync signal?

If fuel and spark both check out individually but the engine still won't fire, the PCM likely isn't getting a clean, correctly-timed signal from both the cam and crank sensors at the same time. This is the stage that gets skipped most often because each sensor can test "fine" in isolation while the sync between them is still off.

  • Confirm both cam and crank sensor connectors are fully seated and pins aren't backed out
  • Check for correct air gap and sensor orientation if either was recently replaced
  • Look for a scan tool code specific to cam/crank correlation, not just a generic sensor fault
  • Rule out a compromised harness section between the sensors and PCM — chafed wire or a bad splice can pass a static test but fail under cranking vibration
From the bench I rebuilt my harness and replaced every major sensor on this exact hunt, and the sync signal between cam and crank was still the last thing standing. If you've already thrown parts at fuel and spark with no luck, stop guessing and go straight to verifying cam/crank sync and ASD relay behavior with a scope or scan tool before buying anything else.
